# CS-255-System-Analysis-Design

### Project Summary
DriverPass is a web-based, cloud-hosted application designed to help students bridge the gap between getting their learner's permits and passing their DMV driving tests. The client, Liam, had a mission to help more students pass their driving tests. He wanted to accomplish this by allowing students to select different packages that provide online classes, practice tests, and on-the-road training with driving instructors. The system I designed allows students to easily schedule their reservations for driver training lessons online and track their progress via a personalized online dashboard.

### Strengths in System Design
My biggest strength in these projects was translating the complex system requirements gathered from a raw interview transcript into clean, legible, and organized design diagrams. Coming from an engineering background, I found it pretty intuitive to map out logical database classes, flowcharts with accurate validation loops, and sequence flows. I was able to accurately and efficiently show the complex logic of the system in an organized manner.

### Room for Improvement
If I could revise one part of my work, I would add more detail to the UML models. Due to the time constraints of the project timeline, I didn't go as in-depth as I would have liked. For example, I would have liked to map out specific methods on the class diagrams, or show a more comprehensive web of `<<include>>` and `<<extend>>` conditions on the use case diagrams to make the design more thorough.

### Interpreting User Needs
To implement the user's needs, I went back and reviewed the Business Requirements Document (BRD) and the interview transcript several times. It was pivotal to have the interview written down so I could constantly cross-reference my designs with what the clients actually wanted. That's something I will take with me moving forward into my career! Considering the user's needs is the most important part of system design because a user's adoption of a new tool can be make-or-break based on one annoying hiccup or hurdle. People naturally resist new things, so addressing the users' pain points and making the experience seamless is the only way a system actually succeeds.

### Future Strategy
I actually really enjoyed learning about system modeling. I found that this will be very handy in my day-to-day work. Even as a programmer, I often have to communicate with less technical coworkers, and I have found that UML diagrams are an invaluable tool to help explain complex topics, especially for visual learners. I will definitely keep these in my back pocket. The next time I need to explain a complex feature, I know I can quickly whip up a sequence diagram or activity diagram to show that flow of logic much better than just relying on technical jargon.
